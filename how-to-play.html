01

Overview

MedSim Nepal is a real-time clinical case simulation. You are the treating clinician. A patient arrives at your department. You review their presentation, order investigations, interpret results, form a diagnosis, and initiate management — all within a time-limited, budget-constrained simulation that faithfully models how the patient's condition evolves.

This is not a quiz. There are no multiple choice questions with a single right answer. Clinical reasoning, investigation strategy, and management decisions all interact with each other and with the passage of simulated time. Your patient can deteriorate, develop complications, and die if you delay, misdiagnose, or treat incorrectly.

Every case is set in Nepal, with costs in Nepalese Rupees (NPR), and designed to reflect real disease burdens and clinical presentations seen in South Asian hospitals.

02

Game Flow

Each case follows this structure:

1
Read the Presentation
Review the patient's history, vitals, and examination findings in the History tab. This is your clinical data — study it carefully. Every detail is placed deliberately.
2
Order Investigations
Select targeted tests from the Investigations tab. Tests cost money, take time to return, and results change depending on the disease stage. Order strategically, not reflexively.
3
Review Results
Results appear in the Results tab as they return. Each result includes an interpretation note. Use these to narrow your differential, confirm your diagnosis, and guide management.
4
Reach a Diagnosis
From the Diagnosis & Treatment tab, select your working diagnosis from the differential list. Your diagnosis determines which specific treatment options become available. You can change your diagnosis at any time before ending the case.
5
Initiate Management
Administer general management options (IV fluids, monitoring, analgesia) and diagnosis-specific treatments. Each management action has clinical consequences — beneficial, harmful, or neutral.
6
End the Case
When you're confident, press "End Case". Your performance is scored across five dimensions. The patient's fate depends on whether you identified the correct diagnosis and applied the right curative treatment in time.
03

Difficulty Tiers

Cases are assigned a difficulty level reflecting the level of clinical training expected to solve them comfortably. Higher difficulty cases feature more overlapping differentials, subtler clinical clues, tighter budgets, and faster disease progression.

Intern
Intern / House Officer
Classic presentations with clear discriminating features. Limited differential overlap. Designed to build foundational reasoning skills. Budget generous.
MO
Medical Officer
Moderate differential overlap. Some trap diagnoses. Results require pattern recognition. Budget requires selective test ordering.
Resident
Resident / PGY 1–3
Complex presentations with multiple trap differentials. Tests have evolving results. Management consequences are more pronounced. Time pressure elevated.
Senior
Senior Resident / Consultant
Atypical presentations, rare diagnoses, fine management distinctions. Maximum budget pressure. Designed for senior trainees and faculty calibration.
04

Time & Disease Progression

Simulated time advances automatically. Each case has a built-in progression timer — if the patient's condition is not addressed, the disease advances through successive stages, each more serious than the last.

Time is measured in simulated hours. Investigations take time to return (from 0.3 hours for a urine dipstick to 24+ hours for a culture). Disease stages advance independently of investigation timelines.

Key time mechanics:

  • Each disease stage has an afterTime — the number of simulated hours before it advances to the next stage if no appropriate treatment is given.
  • Correct general management options add a delayProgression bonus — buying additional hours before the next stage transition.
  • Correct curative treatment applied in time prevents progression entirely.
  • The "Jump Time" button advances the clock manually — useful to retrieve pending investigation results, but at the cost of disease progression risk.
Critical principle: In time-sensitive conditions (STEMI, septic shock, surgical emergencies), acting before investigation results return is clinically appropriate — and rewarded. Don't wait for every test to come back if the diagnosis is clinically clear.
05

Budget & Costs

Every case begins with a fixed budget in NPR (Nepalese Rupees), typically NPR 3,000–8,000 depending on difficulty. All investigations and management options have associated costs in NPR, reflecting approximate real-world costs in Nepali tertiary hospitals.

Budget is a hard constraint. Spending more than your budget results in:

  • A Cost Efficiency score of zero (15 points lost)
  • Potential forced constraint on further investigations

Strategy: Order only what will meaningfully change your management. Order confirmatory tests after strong clinical suspicion is formed. Avoid "dummy" tests (those the engine flags as non-contributory for this case) unless you have budget to spare.

The optimal play is a targeted workup within 50–70% of the available budget, leaving room for treatment costs.

06

Disease Stages

Every case has at least 3 clinical stages plus a terminal stage. Stage names are deliberately non-spoiling — they describe the clinical state, not the diagnosis or the specific pathological process. Stages are colour-coded by severity:

● Yellow
Initial Stage
Stable. The presenting condition. Adequate window for workup and early treatment. Lowest urgency.
● Orange
Worsening Stage
Clinical deterioration. New symptoms. Vital derangement. Urgency elevated. Some tests become less useful or unavailable.
● Red
Critical Stage
Haemodynamic compromise, systemic failure, or surgical emergency. Major penalties. Limited management options.
■ Black
Fatal Outcome
Death or critical transfer. Game over. The case ends with an educational post-mortem message.

When a stage transition occurs, a stage flash overlay appears with a brief clinical message explaining what has happened pathophysiologically. This is an educational moment — read it.

Stage transitions also add new active symptoms and alter vital signs, which may give you additional clinical clues even if progression is unwanted.

07

Vitals System

The patient's vital signs are displayed in the HUD header at all times and evolve with disease progression and management interventions.

Monitored vitals: Heart Rate (HR), Blood Pressure (BP), Temperature (Temp), Respiratory Rate (RR), SpO₂.

When vitals reach clinically dangerous thresholds, they display in red with a pulsing animation — a visual alarm.

Management options with a vitalEffect modify vitals directly. For example, IV fluids in dengue critical phase reduce HR and raise BP. Beta-blockers in STEMI reduce HR but are harmful in cardiogenic shock.

Clinical clue: Vital signs at each stage are clinically accurate. A rising haematocrit in dengue, rising HR with a falling BP in sepsis, or a bradycardic patient with an anterior STEMI should all trigger specific diagnostic suspicion.
08

Ordering Tests

The Investigations tab shows all available tests, organised by category. Tests that are unavailable at the current disease stage are greyed out. Tests you have already ordered or received are marked accordingly.

Each test card shows:

  • Test name and full clinical name
  • Cost in NPR
  • Return time in simulated hours
  • Availability status

Results appear automatically in the Results tab once the simulated return time has elapsed. Pending results can be retrieved by using the "Jump Time" feature (which advances the clock). Results that return in a later disease stage will reflect the results appropriate to that stage.

Budget warning: Ordering every available test will exceed your budget and result in zero cost efficiency points. Think before you order — would this result change your management decision?
09

Test Types

Confirmatory
Confirmatory
The gold standard or highly specific test for the correct diagnosis. A positive result in the right clinical context is diagnostic. This is what you're working toward with your differential reasoning.
Useful
Useful
Eliminates differentials when negative, supports the diagnosis when positive, or provides important monitoring data. Most investigations in a case are "useful" — their value depends on which differentials are still live.
Dummy
Dummy
Ordered commonly in clinical practice but contributes nothing to this specific case. Results are non-discriminatory. Ordering these wastes budget. The engine reveals the dummy classification after the result returns.

Test results change meaningfully across stages. A test that is confirmatory at Stage 1 may become less sensitive by Stage 3 (e.g., NS1 antigen in dengue drops after Day 5). A normal chest X-ray at Stage 1 may show pulmonary oedema at Stage 3. Stage-aware test interpretation is part of the clinical skill being trained.

10

Reading Results

Every completed test shows two pieces of information:

  • Result text — the raw clinical result, formatted as it would appear on a lab report or imaging report.
  • Interpretation — an educational note explaining what this result means in the context of this case, which differentials it supports or eliminates, and what clinical action it should prompt.

The interpretation is your teaching moment. It explains why the result looks the way it does, references the relevant textbook principle, and flags when results are misleading, stage-dependent, or require correlation with other findings.

11

Reaching a Diagnosis

Each case has 7–10 diagnosis options — a curated differential list including the correct diagnosis, trap diagnoses sharing multiple clinical features, and plausible-but-incorrect alternatives.

You select your working diagnosis from the Diagnosis & Treatment tab. This determines which disease-specific management options are displayed. You can change your selected diagnosis at any time before ending the case — changing your diagnosis does not automatically change any management already administered.

The correct diagnosis is not always listed first. The list is not ordered by likelihood. Clinical reasoning from the presentation and test results is required.

Key mechanic: Selecting the wrong diagnosis and administering its curative treatment will either have no effect on the patient's actual condition (neutral) or worsen it (harmful). The simulation models pathophysiological accuracy — treating dengue as scrub typhus with doxycycline does nothing; treating it with chloramphenicol worsens thrombocytopenia.
12

Management Options

Management is divided into two categories:

  • General Management — supportive interventions that apply regardless of diagnosis (IV fluids, monitoring, oxygen, analgesia, antipyretics). These slow disease progression and stabilise the patient but do not cure.
  • Disease-Specific Management — treatments that are appropriate for the selected diagnosis. These range from curative (if the diagnosis is correct) to harmful (if the diagnosis is wrong).

Once a management option is administered, it cannot be undone. Its effects (beneficial, neutral, or harmful) are applied immediately to the game state.

Management options are not labelled with their type in-game — you must use clinical knowledge to determine what is appropriate. The type classification is revealed in the end-of-case results screen.

13

Treatment Types Explained

Every management option in the engine has an assigned type. These are not visible during gameplay — you must reason to them. They determine what happens when you administer the option.

✦ Curative + Supportive ~ Partial ✕ Wrong ⚠ Blunder $ Unnecessary ⏳ Delaying
✦ Curative
Directly addresses the pathological process. Only cures if the selected diagnosis is correct. Applying a curative treatment for the wrong diagnosis has no curative effect.
+ Supportive
Appropriate adjunct or symptomatic treatment. Doesn't cure but helps stabilise, delay progression, and improve patient comfort. Scored positively if appropriate.
~ Partial
Marginally beneficial — may slow progression slightly but misses the core treatment need. Acceptable if correct treatment is also given; insufficient alone.
✕ Wrong
Appropriate for the selected (wrong) diagnosis but neutral or mildly harmful to the actual patient. No progression of actual disease; mild penalty.
⚠ Blunder
Clinically dangerous regardless of diagnosis. Causes direct harm, significant penalties, and may trigger immediate game-ending outcomes. The engine reserves this for treatments with no defensible rationale in context (e.g., NSAIDs in dengue with thrombocytopenia).
$ Unnecessary
Expensive option with no clinical benefit for this patient. Wastes budget. Small or no penalty but significantly impacts Cost Efficiency scoring.
⏳ Delaying
Creates false reassurance. May appear superficially reasonable but does nothing clinically meaningful. Gives the appearance of action while progression continues. Common in cases where learners reach for "observe and wait".
14

General Management

General management options are available regardless of your selected diagnosis and are appropriate in most clinical contexts:

  • IV access and monitoring
  • IV fluids (crystalloids per appropriate protocol)
  • Oxygen therapy (when indicated)
  • Antipyretics (Paracetamol — not NSAIDs in febrile illness with thrombocytopenia risk)
  • Antiemetics, analgesia (appropriate to context)
  • NPO if surgical or procedural case
  • Monitoring protocols (vitals, urine output, serial labs)

Each general management option has a stageEffect — a specific clinical outcome description and progression delay for each active stage. These are shown after administration.

Some general management options are themselves contextually harmful — for example, IV morphine in cardiogenic shock, or aggressive IV fluids in a case of pulmonary oedema. The engine models these consequences accurately.

15

Scoring Rubric

Your final score is calculated across five dimensions, totalling 100 base points plus penalties:

Dimension Description Points
Correct Diagnosis You identified the correct diagnosis from the differential list +25
Timely Cure Correct curative treatment was administered before significant progression. Full points if cure achieved in Stage 1; partial if Stage 2. +30
Cost Efficiency Total spend relative to budget. Full points for ≤70% spend; graded down above; zero if over budget. +15
Correct Tests Ratio of useful/confirmatory tests ordered vs dummy tests ordered. Penalised for excessive dummy tests. +15
Correct Management Match of administered treatments against the case's ideal management list. Includes appropriate general management and disease-specific supportive care. +15
Penalties Each penalty point deducted from total. Accumulates from blunders, wrong management, unnecessary treatment, and stage transitions. −1 per pt
16

Penalties

Penalties accumulate throughout the case and are subtracted from your base score at the end. Common penalty triggers:

  • Stage progression without treatment — each stage advance carries an educational penalty reflecting avoidable harm
  • Administering a blunder treatment — immediate penalty ranging from 10–50 points
  • Ordering investigations after cure — wasteful spending post-recovery
  • Wrong management in disease-specific section — 5–20 points depending on harm level
  • Fatal blunders — may trigger immediate end-game (death outcome)

The penalty system is calibrated so that a learner who makes reasonable clinical decisions — even if not perfectly optimal — achieves a passing score. Blunders and systematic errors are what drive scores into the failing range.

17

Grade Thresholds

🏆
Perfect — ≥90%
Correct diagnosis, early curative treatment, targeted investigations, ideal management, no blunders. The textbook case.
✓
Good — 70–89%
Correct diagnosis and cure. Minor inefficiencies — perhaps slightly delayed treatment, a few unnecessary tests, or suboptimal (but not harmful) management choices.
⚠
Average — 50–69%
Patient survived but with complications. Diagnosis reached late, excess spending, or some wrong management administered without cure being timely.
✕
Poor — <50%
Patient died or suffered severe complications. Significant diagnostic or management failures. Educational post-case feedback provided.
18

Strategy Tips

🔬
Narrow before ordering
Form a differential of 3–4 diagnoses from the presentation alone. Order only tests that discriminate between them. Don't order a battery hoping something will light up.
⏱
Act on the ECG, not the troponin
In STEMI-type emergencies, the ECG is diagnostic. Waiting for all results before treating time-sensitive conditions is a penalised behaviour.
💉
General management first
Start IV access, monitoring, and appropriate general management early. These provide progression delay while you form your diagnosis. They are almost always correct to give.
🧪
Read the interpretation
Every test result has an interpretation note. These explain the pathophysiology, textbook basis, and differential implications. They are the primary educational content of the platform.
💰
Leave budget for treatment
Investigations and treatment both cost money. A thrombolysis or primary PCI can cost NPR 1,500–3,500. If you've spent all your budget on tests, you may not be able to afford the cure.
📋
The clue is real
Every case has a highlighted "Clinical Clue" — a key finding or reasoning pearl that distinguishes the correct diagnosis. Find it, and you'll often crack the case without running every test.
Remember: MedSim Nepal is a learning tool. The end-of-case screen shows exactly what the ideal management was, which treatments were correct or harmful, and why. A poor score is an opportunity to learn — not a reflection of your value as a future clinician.
